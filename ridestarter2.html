WEB APPLICATION | BLOC PROJECT | CASE STUDY

Ridestarter is a crowdfunding website application for organizations and individuals looking to either raise funds or donate to charity bike rides across the world. Users can choose to create a bike campaign, supporting specific causes of their choosing or browse and donate through the different active bike campaigns.

Problem Statement

A search for Charity bike challenges

The net market exists several popular crowdfunding services such as GoFundme, Kickstarter, and Indiegogo. However, many of these web services either cater to holistic campaigns or specific ones such as creative projects. When users want to create a charity bike ride campaign, they would need to host their campaign through either the organization they're funding for or a general crowd-sourced site. Unfortunately, there isn't a centralized crowdsourced site for charity bike rides.

Solution Overview

A Trusted Crowdfunding Medium

Ridestarter seeks to consolidate charity bike rides and allow users to crowd-sourced for ride projects in one central location. The goal for Ridestarter is to enable donors and fundraisers to come together in supporting charity rides for causes they care about. Donors can browse and donate to a specific cause of their choosing while fundraisers can effortlessly build bike projects through an intuitive creation flow.

Discovery & Strategy Phase

Getting to know my Users

At this initial stage, it was crucial to gain a better understanding of users' behavior of donating to a charity or spearheading a fundraiser. The survey was aimed towards a group of people who have made donations, created fundraiser projects, participated in charity bike ride and also users who have neither fundraised or donated.

  • Type of donation users have made out to
  • Reasons for why users donate
  • Reasons for why users don't donate
  • User donation habits
  • Frustrations when managing a fundraiser

Interviewing my Users

In addition to sending out the survey, I held user interviews with both users who have donated and fundraised. It was also important to target users who have been on charity bike rides to gain a deeper understanding of the pain points when creating a bike campaign.

I rather donate to people or organizations that I can connect personally with
It would be cool to get potential riders to participate in the charity
Donating to smaller charities such as giving a dollar to a struggling kid is more impactful than funding a larger organization

Research Insights

1. Often time, users are hesitant to donate online due to not knowing where their money is going to. I sought to answer how might we create trust and transparency to debunk hesitation and build a rapport with the users.

2. From a fundraiser's perspective, the biggest pain point was marketing and how to incentivize users outside of the riders' mutual circle to donate to a campaign.

Constructing my Audiences

Following the user survey and interviews, I used the feedback as the jumping off point to create user personas. I was able to craft and hone two relevant personas: bargain hunter - one who spends an exorbitant amount of time looking for the best deals and power-shopper - one who is driven by a specific need and know specifically what they want. With the personas, I was able to bring more empathy to the early phases and eventually throughout the project.

Information Architecture Phase

Distilling the Flow Process

Before touching on the wireframes, I wanted to ensure the clarity of Ridestarter's goals and structures. To do that, I created a detailed sitemap from the start of the landing page to the different elements of a crowdfunding site.

User Flow

Using the user stories as a guide to deliver a sufficient user flow, I focused on the development of both donors and fundraisers alike.

Developing the Initial Screens

To show potential users how the application is supposed to function and be built, I designed the low-fidelity wireframes in Balsamiq based on the user stories. It was also essential to remember to develop from both the donators and fundraisers' experience. Given that mind, I created wireframes from the fundraiser's perspective where they can manage and edit their projects.

Visual Identity Phase

Cycling through the Brand Identity

With Ridestarter being a crowdfunding application, eliciting trust and emotion are the most important facet of getting users to use the site. The color palette was comprised mainly of the color green. Green was used as the primary color for Ridestarter because not only green conveys with money and funds, but also promotes an association for nature and foliages, which are all presented during a rider's journey.

Prototyping Phase

Finalizing the Crowdfunding Screens

Following the completion and testing of the low-fidelity wireframes, I began on the construction of the high-fidelity wireframes.

Charity Page View

The main charity page follows a minimum viable product based on the user research. Essential elements are displayed to ease the users in interacting with a bike charity page.

Filtering and Sorting System

With many crowdfunding projects out there, this streamlined filtering and sorting system allow for users to tailor down the specific projects of their choice.

Conveying Reputation

Based on the user research, one of the leading factors in users not committing to a donation is being unsure of the fundraiser's trustworthiness. Donors must feel safe donating to creators, so it's important to also focus on the project owner's credibility.

Usability Phase

Building Trust through Transparency

Disclosing reputation and personal information was important for potential donors to empathize with the fundraisers and make a donation. However, I knew I was struggling with users intuitively navigating to the fundraiser's profile page.

Click Test Result

By gathering more than 50 click test responses, my assumption was wrong as a majority of testers successfully clicked on the correct element (creator's image), despite a few outliers. However, I wanted to emphasize the fundraiser's reputation further. So I added another profile link avenue by creating a small button under the fundraiser's name to view their profile.

Reflections

1. Ridestarter has a value in allowing users to exclusively search and donate to charity bike rides, solving the problem of transparency in crowd-sourcing charity projects. Building an application with the goal to get people to not only get excited to donate money, but also create trust and openness in the platform requires thoughtful decisions in the design process. What’s difficult, but significant was coming up with the right questions to ask in the user survey to uncover underlying goals and motives. The interview process was a humbling experience in learning more about the different perspective from both the donators and fundraisers. Asking questions and empathizing to explore deeper into the users’ mind was an experience that allowed me to reflect on what is considered a good user interview process.

2. An area for improvement with Ridestarter would be to research more on what to include in a charity ride crowdfunding platform - what is needed to either get people interested in riding or donating to ride projects. The good news is that an application is never a final product as design and iteration is an ongoing process. I hope to further improve on the aesthetic and functionality of Ridestarter to create a good crowdfunding user experience.
