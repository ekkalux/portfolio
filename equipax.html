WEB APPLICATION | BLOC PROJECT | CASE STUDY

Equipax is an eccomerce web application for outdoor backpacks. Users can select an outdoor activity and purchase premium backpacks based on their desired choices.

Problem Statement

Shopping online isn't the same as in Store

Surely there are ecommerce sites that offer outdoor gears for the thrill-seekers. Even with these shopping sites, their items focus on a general selection of apparels and gears - with backpacks specifically hidden in their many choices. Coupling with some of the unnecessary flows to purchasing an online product.

Solution Overview

Replicating similar Shopping experiences

Equipax aims to provide users with a seamless shopping experience when they are making their backpack purchases. Users are provided with an all traditional element of an eccomerce site with a curtail focus on outdoor backpacks. Users can easily compare items and contact live support agents should they have questions. The solution overall aims to mirror an in-store experience for backpack enthusiasts, designed to establish a connection with shoppers.

Discovery & Strategy Phase

Getting Personal with my Users

To kick off my research, I surveyed many different types of users. From outdoor enthusiasts to general shoppers, I wanted to find out about the following users habits and perspectives in the following areas:

  • How do users shop for backpacks?
  • Which backpack features are the most important?
  • What activities do users partake in?
  • Online shopping habits
  • Frustrations when shopping online

Interviewing my Users

To supplement my research, I sat down with four different users to discuss about their shopping habits and what drives them to shop online. It was very important to dig deep in different angles as to what would provide a good shopping experience for potential users.

Research Insights

1. Most users shop for backpacks mainly for its functionality. Although style and colors add to the appeal, it seems to be less important than price and the practicality of a product.

2. Users are very caution of online shopping due to its inconsistency between online and in-store impression. Mirroring an in-store experience is very important for users to selecting the right product.

3. Often time, users are met with a wide selection of items. This can be overwhelming for shoppers, creating an uncertainty of purchasing an item with the right balance in price and quality.

Who is up for an adventure?

Following the user survey and interviews, I used the feedback as the jumping off point to create user personas. I was able to craft and hone two relevant personas: bargain hunter - one who spends an exorbitant amount of time looking for the best deals and power-shopper - one who are driven by a specific need and know exactly what they want. With the personas, I was able to bring more empathy to the early phases and eventually throughout the project.

Information Architecture Phase

Maintaining a Minimum Viable Product

Using Google Sheets and Draw.io, I created both user stories and user flows based on my research to develop a minimum viable product. For both new and returning users, being able to find a specific backpack and knowing where to complete a purchase are the most critical to building Equipax.

Crafting the Origin Wireframes

Keeping the research in mind, I used Sketch to produce medium-fidelity wireframes. After completing the wireframes, I presented the mockups to three potential users. I wanted to focus on making sure that the screen flow caters to my personas' needs. Specifically I wanted to ensure that users can successfully scout for a backpack and purchase it through the end.

Visual Identity Phase

Evoking Outdoorsman through Branding

To begin the branding process, I needed a name for the product. Going through several iterations of mind-mapping, I eventually landed on Equipax. "Equip" and "backpack" were synthesized to form Equipax, simply meaning to put on a backpack. To further the logo, I vecterized the letter Q to resemble the two straps on a backpack and assigned it with the brand's two primary colors.

Logo Design

When building the visual identity for Equipax, I wanted to convey an outdoor feel while maintaining a clean ecommerence design. The maroon and green are used as the primary colors. The yellow color is used both as an accent to complement the main colors and as a call to action. The typeface - Avenir - is intended to emphasize a modern look with different angles of kerning and spacing for headings and paragraphs.

Usability Phase

Mirroring an In-Store Experience

To evaluate whether users have easy accessibility to a live support agent, I conducted a click test and was able to gather 21 responses. Based on the test, users had no trouble finding the live chat button.

Additional User Testing

1. Testers were met with positive interaction with Equipax. Users can clearly identify the navigation flow from the start of finding a backpack to completing a successful purchase.

2. Consistency is also very important when going through the purchasing screens of an item where a lot of information are presented. A second promo code registration option was added to the Shipping/Payment page when users hinted that they couldn't add a promo code unless having to go back to the cart page.
